My Strategies for Asking Good Questions

12/7/14

Asking questions is always a crucial part of my learning process and I try to do it well. Towards that end, I try to remember the following points when crafting questions and interacting with people who are trying to answer my questions:

1.) State the question clearly. I type it out, pause for a moment, and then reread it. Could someone else instantly understand what I am asking? Is it grammatically correct? Is the spelling correct? Do I supply enough context for the question to make sense?

2.) Regarding supplying enough context, I try to be extremely aware of the mutual information shared by myself and the person who is answering my question. With a good friend asking "Are we up?" is enough of a question for him to tell me the current score of the game we are both interested in. This is because we have a largely overlapping field of interest and a lot of mutual information. But posing this same question to a different person with less shared knowledge and perspective will only illicit a puzzled look. To avoid this, it is necessary to take into account the perspective of the person and supply sufficient context so they can understand the question easily.

3.) State what you are trying to accomplish in the end. When asking a question about how to accomplish something, particularly a question about programming, I always try to state what I am attempting to accomplish. If I have a problem making a given method work it is often the case that I am approaching the larger problem in an inefficient or impossible way. By stating my final aim, it allows more knowledgeable people to recommend local improvements but also better global approaches.

4.) Remember to be appreciative and polite. The people answering my questions are doing me a big favor and I want to show them the respect this favor merits.
